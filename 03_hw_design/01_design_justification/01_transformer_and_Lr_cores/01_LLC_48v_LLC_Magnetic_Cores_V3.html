Bpeak formula

$$ B_{peak} = \frac{L_m \cdot Im_{peak}}{N_p \cdot A_e}= \frac{L_m \cdot \sqrt{2} \cdot Im_{rms}}{N_p \cdot A_e} $$

Where

  • A_e = effective core area (in m²)
  • N_p = primary turns

The Al value:

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Al}_{nH} &= 1 \times 10 ^ {3} \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{Lm}_{uH} }{ \left( n \right) ^{ 2 } } \\&= 1 \times 10 ^ {3} \cdot \frac{ 65.392 }{ \left( 4.000 \right) ^{ 2 } } \\&= 4087.000 \; \;\textrm{(nH/turn squared)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

We can reduce $A_L$ by adding an air gap, below the formula of air gap length

$$ A_{L}' = \frac{A_L}{1 + \mu_r \,\frac{L_g}{L_e}} $$$$ l_g = \frac{L_e}{\mu_r} \left( \frac{A_L}{A_L'} - 1 \right) $$

Where

  • $ A_{L}' $ : the corrected inductance factor (in nH or µH), after introducing the air gap
  • $ A_L $ : the initial inductance factor provided by the core manufacturer (in nH or µH)
  • $ l_g $ : the air gap length (in mm)
  • $ L_e $ : the effective magnetic path length of the core (in mm)

Core ELP 64/10/50 with I 64/5/50 ¶

Inputs data¶

μ_r: relative permeability (material property) μ_e: effective permeability (assembly/shape property)
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{core} &= \mathrm{} \; \;\textrm{(ELP 64/10/50 with I 64/5/50)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Al}_{nH} &= 14000 \; \;\textrm{(nH)} \\[10pt] A_{e} &= 519 \; \;\textrm{(mm2)} \\[10pt] A_{e_{min}} &= 518 \; \;\textrm{(mm2)} \\[10pt] l_{e} &= 69.700 \; \;\textrm{(mm)} \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mu_{r} &= 1450 \; \;\textrm{(N87)} \\[10pt] B_{sat} &= 300 \; \;\textrm{(mT @ 250A/m 10kHz 100°C)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{K1} &= 835 \; \;\textrm{(@ 25°C, 0.10 mm < s < 2.00 mm)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{K2} &= -0.790 \; \;\textrm{(@ 25°C, 0.10 mm < s < 2.00 mm)} \\[10pt] \mu_{0} &= 4 \cdot \pi \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-7} = 4 \cdot 3.142 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-7} &= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(H m−1)} \end{aligned} $

The air gap¶

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Lm}_{nH} &= \mathrm{Lm}_{uH} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {3} = 65.392 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {3} &= 65392.000 \; \;\textrm{(nH)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Al}_{nH} &= 14000 \; \;\textrm{(nH)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Al}_{Target} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Lm}_{nH} }{ \left( n \right) ^{ 2 } } = \frac{ 65392.000 }{ \left( 4.000 \right) ^{ 2 } } &= 4087.000 \; \;\textrm{(nH)} \\[10pt] \mu_{eTarget} &= \mu_{r} \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{Al}_{Target} }{ \mathrm{Al}_{nH} } = 1450 \cdot \frac{ 4087.000 }{ 14000 } &= 423.296 \end{aligned} $

Simified formula of air gap

See the formula above

$ \begin{aligned} l_{g} &= 1 \times 10 ^ {3} \cdot \left( \frac{ \mu_{0} \cdot \left( n \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot A_{e} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} }{ \mathrm{Lm} } - l_{e} \cdot \frac{ 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} }{ \mu_{r} } \right) \\&= 1 \times 10 ^ {3} \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.000 \cdot \left( 4.000 \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 519 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} }{ 0.000 } - 69.700 \cdot \frac{ 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} }{ 1450 } \right) \\&= 0.112 \; \;\textrm{(mm)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

TDK formula of air gap using K1 and K2

Figure 1: The empirical air-gap formula using the coefficients K1 and K2 from the TDK datasheet

See page 6 [1].

$ \begin{aligned} s_{mm} &= \left( \frac{ \mathrm{Al}_{Target} }{ \mathrm{K1} } \right) ^{ \left( \frac{ 1 }{ \mathrm{K2} } \right) } \\&= \left( \frac{ 4087.000 }{ 835 } \right) ^{ \left( \frac{ 1 }{ -0.790 } \right) } \\&= 0.134 \; \;\textrm{(mm airgap)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

TDK air gap curve

Figure 2: The empirical air-gap formula using the coefficients K1 and K2 from the TDK datasheet

See page 2 [2].

$ \begin{aligned} l_{g_{curve}} &= 0.140 \; \;\textrm{(mm)} \end{aligned} $

Air gap using the tree methods

$ \begin{aligned} s_{mm} &= 0.134 \; \;\textrm{(mm using TDK formula K1, K2)} \\[10pt] l_{g} &= 0.112 \; \;\textrm{(mm using the simple formula)} \\[10pt] l_{g_{curve}} &= 0.140 \; \;\textrm{(mm using TDK graph)} \end{aligned} $

The three results are close to each other; we will prefer the TDK formula using K1 and K2 if these coefficients are provided in the datasheet.

The Bpeak and Bsat¶

Reluctance formulas

$ \begin{aligned} \mu_{e} &= \frac{ l_{e} + l_{g} }{ l_{g} + \frac{ l_{e} }{ \mu_{r} } } = \frac{ 69.700 + 0.112 }{ 0.112 + \frac{ 69.700 }{ 1450 } } &= 437.476 \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} R_{core} &= l_{e} \cdot \frac{ 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} }{ \mu_{0} \cdot \mu_{r} \cdot A_{e} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 69.700 \cdot \frac{ 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} }{ 0.000 \cdot 1450 \cdot 519 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 73703.405 \\[10pt] \\[10pt] R_{gap} &= 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot \frac{ l_{g} }{ \mu_{0} \cdot A_{e} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot \frac{ 0.112 }{ 0.000 \cdot 519 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 170974.843 \\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} R_{tot} &= R_{core} + R_{gap} \\&= 73703.405 + 170974.843 \\&= 244678.248 \; \;\textrm{(At/Wb, using Rcore, Rgap)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] R_{tot_{2}} &= 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot \frac{ l_{g} + l_{e} }{ \mu_{0} \cdot \mu_{e} \cdot A_{e} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot \frac{ 0.112 + 69.700 }{ 0.000 \cdot 437.476 \cdot 519 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 244678.248 \; \;\textrm{(At/Wb, using µe)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Im}_{sat} &= B_{sat} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot A_{e} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \cdot \frac{ R_{core} + R_{gap} }{ n } \\&= 300 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot 519 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \cdot \frac{ 73703.405 + 170974.843 }{ 4.000 } \\&= 9.524 \; \;\textrm{(A)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Im}_{peak} &= \sqrt { 2 } \cdot \mathrm{Im}_{rms} = \sqrt { 2 } \cdot 6.992 &= 9.888 \; \;\textrm{(A)} \end{aligned} $

$ I_{m_{peak}} > I_{m_{sat}} $, let’s double-check our hypothesis and margins.

Verification of the Hypothesis¶

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Vf} &= 0.200 \; \;\textrm{(drop voltage in the mos)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{efficiency} &= 0.950 \; \;\textrm{(Hypothesis, efficiency= 95\%)} \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{loss} &= \frac{ 1 - \mathrm{efficiency} }{ 3 } \\&= \frac{ 1 - 0.950 }{ 3 } \\&= 0.017 \; \;\textrm{(Hypothesis: Joule loss = 1/3 of total losses)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Io}_{nom} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Power} }{ \mathrm{Vo}_{nom} } = \frac{ 1200.000 }{ 48.000 } &= 25.000 \; \;\textrm{(A)} \\[10pt] V_{loss} &= \frac{ \frac{ \mathrm{Power} \cdot \mathrm{loss} }{ \mathrm{efficiency} } }{ \mathrm{Io}_{nom} } = \frac{ \frac{ 1200.000 \cdot 0.017 }{ 0.950 } }{ 25.000 } &= 0.842 \; \;\textrm{(v)} \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{margin} &= 0.010 \; \\[10pt] \mathrm{Mg}_{max} &= n \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{Vo}_{max} \cdot \left( 1 + \mathrm{margin} \right) + \mathrm{Vf} + V_{loss} }{ \frac{ V_{In_{min}} }{ 2 } } \\&= 4.000 \cdot \frac{ 54.000 \cdot \left( 1 + 0.010 \right) + 0.200 + 0.842 }{ \frac{ 360.000 }{ 2 } } \\&= 1.235 \\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Mg}_{max110} &= \mathrm{Mg}_{max} \cdot \left( \frac{ 110 }{ 100 } \right) = 1.235 \cdot \left( \frac{ 110 }{ 100 } \right) &= 1.359 \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{fmin}_{2} &= 75000.000 \; \\[10pt] \omega_{min} &= 471238.898 \; \\[10pt] \mathrm{Im}_{rms2} &= 2 \cdot \sqrt { 2 } \cdot n \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{Vo}_{nom} }{ \pi \cdot \mathrm{Lm} \cdot \omega_{min} } \\&= 2 \cdot \sqrt { 2 } \cdot 4.000 \cdot \frac{ 48.000 }{ 3.142 \cdot 0.000 \cdot 471238.898 } \\&= 5.610 \; \;\textrm{(Arms)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Im}_{peak2} &= \sqrt { 2 } \cdot \mathrm{Im}_{rms2} = \sqrt { 2 } \cdot 5.610 &= 7.933 \; \;\textrm{(A)} \\[10pt] I_{satMargin} &= 100 \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{Im}_{sat} - \mathrm{Im}_{peak2} }{ \mathrm{Im}_{sat} } = 100 \cdot \frac{ 9.524 - 7.933 }{ 9.524 } &= 16.704 \; \;\textrm{(\%)} \end{aligned} $

We can calculate also $B_{peak2}$ using $Im_{rms2}$

$ \begin{aligned} B_{sat} &= 300 \; \;\textrm{(mT)} \\[10pt] B_{peak2} &= 1 \times 10 ^ {3} \cdot \mathrm{Lm} \cdot \sqrt { 2 } \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{Im}_{rms2} }{ n \cdot A_{e_{min}} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 1 \times 10 ^ {3} \cdot 0.000 \cdot \sqrt { 2 } \cdot \frac{ 5.610 }{ 4.000 \cdot 518 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } \\&= 250.369 \; \;\textrm{(mT < Bs= 300mT OK)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

We calculate the margin for $B_{peak2}$

$ \begin{aligned} B_{satMargin} &= 100 \cdot \frac{ B_{sat} - B_{peak2} }{ B_{sat} } \\&= 100 \cdot \frac{ 300 - 250.369 }{ 300 } \\&= 16.544 \; \;\textrm{(\%)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

The calculation $I_{m,\text{sat}} / I_{m,\text{peak}}$ and $B_{\text{peak}} / B_{\text{sat}}$ are equivalent, see the result below

$ \begin{aligned} I_{satMargin} &= 16.704 \; \;\textrm{(\%)} \\[10pt] B_{satMargin} &= 16.544 \; \;\textrm{(\%)} \end{aligned} $

In general, PCB copper is specified in ounces (oz). This can be converted to thickness using a simple formulas as below:

$ \begin{aligned} M_{density} &= 2 \; \;\textrm{(oz/ft2, PCB layer surface mass density)} \\[10pt] M_{density2} &= M_{density} \cdot 28.3495 = 2 \cdot 28.3495 &= 56.699 \; \;\textrm{(g/ft2 (1 oz = 28.3495g))} \\[10pt] M_{density3} &= \frac{ M_{density2} }{ \left( 30.48 \right) ^{ 2 } } = \frac{ 56.699 }{ \left( 30.48 \right) ^{ 2 } } &= 0.061 \; \;\textrm{(g/cm2 (1ft = 30,48 cm))} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Thickness}_{cm} &= \frac{ M_{density3} }{ 8.96 } = \frac{ 0.061 }{ 8.96 } &= 0.007 \; \;\textrm{(cm, copper density 8,96 g/cm³)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Thickness}_{um} &= \mathrm{Thickness}_{cm} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {4} = 0.007 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {4} &= 68.114 \; \;\textrm{(µm)} \end{aligned} $
