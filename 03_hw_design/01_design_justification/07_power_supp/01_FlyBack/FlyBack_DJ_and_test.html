The PWM controller¶

Introduction of the TNY2XX familly¶

We will use a TNY2xx family controller

Why:

  • This controller has an integrated MOSFET
  • It requires a minimum BOM
  • It can operate without an auxiliary winding
  • It provides integrated frequency jitter (spread spectrum to reduce EMI perturbation)

How this controller works ():

  • This controller works with max-current / max-duty-cycle

  • It has an internal clock that fixes the maximum switching frequency

    • example: 132 kHz

  • In each cycle, it checks the feedback pin

    • if the feedback pin is activated → skip (escape) the cycle
    • otherwise → turn on the MOSFET

  • The MOSFET turns off when one of the following conditions is met:

    • the primary current reaches the maximum value
      • example: 250 mA
    • or the duty cycle reaches the maximum allowed value
      • example: 62%

  • This controller has an internal 5.8 V generator

    • used for self-powering
    • no external auxiliary supply is required

For more information pease check the datasheet below [1].



In this design, we will shose the TNY-264 in open frame configuration

  • Breakdown Voltage = 700
  • Power = 9W
  • Frequancy = 132kHz with +/-8kHz of jitter
  • Rds(ON) = 28Ω @ 25°C and 42Ω @ 100°C (typ), 48Ω in the worst case
  • Ilim = 250mA (typ)
  • Max duty cycle = 65% (typ)



Let’s estimate the theorical maximum power that this controller can extract

$ \begin{aligned} I_{lim} &= 0.250 \; \;\textrm{(A)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Freq} &= 132000.000 \; \;\textrm{(Hz)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Freq}_{max} &= 140000.000 \; \;\textrm{(Hz)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{DC}_{max} &= 0.650 \; \\[10pt] \mathrm{Vin}_{max} &= 400 \; \;\textrm{(V)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{di} &= I_{lim} - 0 = 0.250 - 0 &= 0.250 \; \;\textrm{(A)} \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{dt} &= \mathrm{DC}_{max} \cdot \left( \frac{ 1 }{ \mathrm{Freq} } \right) \\&= 6.500 \times 10 ^ {-1} \cdot \left( \frac{ 1 }{ 1.320 \times 10 ^ {5} } \right) \\&= 4.924 \times 10 ^ {-6} \; \;\textrm{(s)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] L &= \frac{ \mathrm{Vin}_{max} }{ \frac{ \mathrm{di} }{ \mathrm{dt} } } \\&= \frac{ 400 }{ \frac{ 2.500 \times 10 ^ {-1} }{ 4.924 \times 10 ^ {-6} } } \\&= 7.879 \times 10 ^ {-3} \; \;\textrm{(H because v = L di/dt)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] L_{uH} &= L \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} \\&= 7.879 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} \\&= 7.879 \times 10 ^ {3} \; \;\textrm{(µH)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] E_{cycle} &= 1 \times 10 ^ {6} \cdot \frac{ 1 }{ 2 } \cdot L \cdot \left( I_{lim} \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= 1 \times 10 ^ {6} \cdot \frac{ 1 }{ 2 } \cdot 7.879 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot \left( 2.500 \times 10 ^ {-1} \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= 2.462 \times 10 ^ {2} \; \;\textrm{(µJ)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} P_{inp} &= \mathrm{Freq} \cdot E_{cycle} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \\&= 132000.000 \cdot 246.212 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \\&= 32.500 \; \;\textrm{(W)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{Efficiency} &= 0.800 \; \;\textrm{(hypothesis)} \\[10pt] P_{out} &= P_{inp} \cdot \mathrm{Efficiency} \\&= 32.500 \cdot 0.800 \\&= 26.000 \; \;\textrm{(W > 9W of the datasheet)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

Let's estimate the thermal discipation in this condition (only conduction losses)

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Rds}_{max} &= 48 \; \\[10pt] \mathrm{Erds}_{cycle} &= \left( \frac{ 1 }{ 3 } \right) \cdot \mathrm{Rds}_{max} \cdot \left( I_{lim} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot \mathrm{dt} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} \\&= \left( \frac{ 1 }{ 3 } \right) \cdot 48 \cdot \left( 0.250 \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 0.000 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} \\&= 4.924 \; \;\textrm{(uJ (current is a slope from 0 to I lim))}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{Prds} &= \mathrm{Freq} \cdot \mathrm{Erds}_{cycle} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \\&= 132000.000 \cdot 4.924 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \\&= 0.650 \; \;\textrm{(W)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{Rth}_{JA} &= 45 \; \;\textrm{(°C/W see the datasheet)} \\[10pt] \Delta_{T} &= 29.250 \; \;\textrm{(°C)} \end{aligned} $
  • The ΔT in this condition is relatively low, because in this quick calculation we assume that the only loss inside the IC is the ohmic loss caused by RDS_ON
  • In reality, there are additional losses, such as switching losses (especially at 132 kHz) and the internal power consumption of the controller.

In our design, the maximum output power will be 4 W, so we respect the datasheet.

Simulation for Understanding TNY‑264, TNY‑280 ...¶

To clearly understand how this controller works, I suggest the following step-by-step simulation, beginning with the simplest configuration and gradually introducing more advanced features. The goal is to improve conceptual understanding, not to provide a fully rigorous or exact model of the controller.

You can find all these simplified models in the link.

Level 1 :

At this level, the PWM controller model includes only a master frequency clock (set to 132.45 kHz in the example below) and a current‑limit reset.
Even with this simplified model, the controller is able to perform primary peak‑current control.

Level 2 :

At this stage, the maximum duty‑cycle reset control is added. The controller resets whenever one of the following conditions occurs:

  • The current reaches its maximum target value.
  • The duty cycle exceeds the controller’s maximum allowed value (approximately 63%).

In the first figure below, the reset is always triggered by the maximum current.
For the second figure, the reset is triggered by the maximum duty cycle.

Figure 3: TNY2xx modeling: Level 2, reset triggered by the max-current.

Figure 4: TNY2xx modeling: Level 2, reset triggered by the max-duty cycle.

Level 3 :

At this level, we introduce the feedback control. The feedback is evaluated at the start of each cycle, and if it is active, the current cycle is skipped. This results in fewer cycles, reduced current, and lower voltage until the feedback is deactivated, after which normal operation resumes.

In first time let's focus on the primary side

  • Initial time step:
    $dt = DC_{\text{max}} \cdot \dfrac{1}{\text{Freq}}$

  • Calculate maximum DC current:
    $I_{\text{dcmax}} = \dfrac{V_{\text{in\_nom}} \cdot dt}{L_m}$

  • Conditional calculation:

    • If $I_{\text{dcmax}} < I_{\text{lim}}$:
      $I_{\text{peak}} = I_{\text{dcmax}}$
      $D = DC_{\text{max}}$

    • Else:
      $I_{\text{peak}} = I_{\text{lim}}$
      $dt = \dfrac{L_m \cdot I_{\text{lim}}}{V_{\text{in\_nom}}}$

      $D = \dfrac{dt}{ Freq}$

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Lm} &= \mathrm{AL} \cdot \left( \mathrm{Np} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-9} = 5200 \cdot \left( 50 \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-9} &= 1.300 \times 10 ^ {-2} \; \;\textrm{(H)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{dt} &= \mathrm{DC}_{max} \cdot \left( \frac{ 1 }{ \mathrm{Freq} } \right) = 6.500 \times 10 ^ {-1} \cdot \left( \frac{ 1 }{ 1.320 \times 10 ^ {5} } \right) &= 4.924 \times 10 ^ {-6} \; \;\textrm{(s)} \\[10pt] I_{dcmax} &= \mathrm{Vin}_{nom} \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{dt} }{ \mathrm{Lm} } = 380 \cdot \frac{ 4.924 \times 10 ^ {-6} }{ 1.300 \times 10 ^ {-2} } &= 1.439 \times 10 ^ {-1} \; \;\textrm{(v = L di/dt)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Ipeak} &= 1.439 \times 10 ^ {-1} \; \;\textrm{(since Idcmax < Ilim)} \end{aligned} $

Let's start with Np = 26 turns; it's a good compromise between primary power and Bpeak.

Let's chose an Ns
To avoid saturation of the core, the magnetic flux ($B$) must return to 0 in each cycle. This means that the magnetizing inductance must discharge completely into the secondary before the end of the cycle. Therefore, the final magnetizing current must be 0A.

Below an example with Ns = 2

  • $D:$ The duty cycle corresponding to $T_{on}$ on the primary mosfet
  • $ D_{secondary}$ : The duty cycle corresponding to $T_{on}$ on the secondary diode
  • $ D_{conduction}$ : The sum of the above duty cycles must be less than one
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Np} &= 26 \; \\[10pt] \mathrm{Ns} &= 2 \; \;\textrm{(example)} \\[10pt] n &= \frac{ \mathrm{Np} }{ \mathrm{Ns} } = \frac{ 26 }{ 2 } &= 13.000 \\[10pt] D &= 0.141 \; \;\textrm{(see above table for Np = 26)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Ipeak} &= 0.250 \; \;\textrm{(A see above table for Np = 26)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Lm}_{uH} &= 1622.400 \; \;\textrm{(uH see above table for Np = 26)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Isec}_{peak} &= n \cdot \mathrm{Ipeak} = 13.000 \cdot 0.250 &= 3.250 \\[10pt] \mathrm{dIsec} &= \mathrm{Isec}_{peak} - 0 = 3.250 - 0 &= 3.250 \\[10pt] \mathrm{Vo} &= \mathrm{Vout}_{nom} + \mathrm{Vd} = 12 + 0.400 &= 12.400 \; \;\textrm{(V)} \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Lms}_{uH} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Lm}_{uH} }{ \left( n \right) ^{ 2 } } \\&= \frac{ 1622.400 }{ \left( 13.000 \right) ^{ 2 } } \\&= 9.600 \; \;\textrm{(uH secondary magnetic inductance)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{dtSec}_{us} &= \mathrm{Lms}_{uH} \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{dIsec} }{ \mathrm{Vo} } \\&= 9.600 \cdot \frac{ 3.250 }{ 12.400 } \\&= 2.516 \; \;\textrm{(us reminder v=Ldi/dt)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] D_{secondary} &= \mathrm{dtSec}_{us} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \cdot \mathrm{Freq} \\&= 2.516 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} \cdot 132000.000 \\&= 0.332 \\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} D_{conduction} &= D + D_{secondary} \\&= 0.141 + 0.332 \\&= 0.473 \; \;\textrm{(must be < 1)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{Vrefl} &= \left( \mathrm{Vout}_{nom} + \mathrm{Vd} \right) \cdot n \\&= \left( 12 + 0.400 \right) \cdot 13.000 \\&= 161.200 \; \;\textrm{(V : This voltage stresses the primary MOSFET)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

RCD snubber¶

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Lm}_{uH} &= 1622 \; \\[10pt] \mathrm{Leakage} &= 3.000 \times 10 ^ {-2} \; \;\textrm{(Hypothesis)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Llk1}_{uH} &= \mathrm{Lm}_{uH} \cdot \mathrm{Leakage} \\&= 1622 \cdot 3.000 \times 10 ^ {-2} \\&= 4.866 \times 10 ^ {1} \; \;\textrm{(uH)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

The snubber allowed voltage is

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Vsn} &= V_{maxRating} - \mathrm{Vin}_{max} = 560.000 - 400 &= 160.000 \; \;\textrm{(V)} \end{aligned} $

Chose the snubber resistor Rsn

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Vrefl} &= \left( \mathrm{Vout}_{nom} + \mathrm{Vd} \right) \cdot n \\&= \left( 12 + 0.400 \right) \cdot 3.250 \\&= 40.300 \; \;\textrm{(V)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{FacV} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Vsn} }{ \mathrm{Vsn} - \mathrm{Vrefl} } \\&= \frac{ 160.000 }{ 160.000 - 40.300 } \\&= 1.337 \\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{Rsn} &= \frac{ \left( \mathrm{Vsn} \right) ^{ 2 } }{ 0.5 \cdot \mathrm{Llk1} \cdot \left( \mathrm{Ipeak} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot \mathrm{Freq} \cdot \mathrm{FacV} } \\&= \frac{ \left( 160.000 \right) ^{ 2 } }{ 0.5 \cdot 0.000 \cdot \left( 0.250 \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 132000.000 \cdot 1.337 } \\&= 95415.312 \; \;\textrm{(Ω)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

Input data for the calculation

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Lm} &= 1.622 \times 10 ^ {-3} \; \;\textrm{(H)} &\mathrm{Np} &= 26 \; &\mathrm{Ns} &= 4 \; \\[10pt] \delta_{Vout} &= 1.000 \times 10 ^ {-2} \; \;\textrm{(1 \%)} &\mathrm{Vout}_{nom} &= 12 \; \;\textrm{(v)} &\mathrm{Vd} &= 4.000 \times 10 ^ {-1} \; \;\textrm{(Diode Forward Drop in V)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \Delta_{Vout} &= \delta_{Vout} \cdot \mathrm{Vout}_{nom} = 0.010 \cdot 12 &= 0.120 \; \;\textrm{(V)} \\[10pt] n &= \frac{ \mathrm{Np} }{ \mathrm{Ns} } = \frac{ 26 }{ 4 } &= 6.500 \end{aligned} $

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Ipeak} &= 0.250 \; \;\textrm{(A, Primary fixed by the controller limit)} \end{aligned} $

Calculate the secondary inductance and ON time

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Lms} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Lm} }{ \left( n \right) ^{ 2 } } \\&= \frac{ 1.622 \times 10 ^ {-3} }{ \left( 6.500 \times 10 ^ {0} \right) ^{ 2 } } \\&= 3.839 \times 10 ^ {-5} \; \;\textrm{(secondary magnetic inductance, H)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{Is}_{peak} &= n \cdot \mathrm{Ipeak} \\&= 6.500 \times 10 ^ {0} \cdot 2.500 \times 10 ^ {-1} \\&= 1.625 \times 10 ^ {0} \; \;\textrm{(A , secondary)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{dIsec} &= \mathrm{Is}_{peak} - 0 \\&= 1.625 \times 10 ^ {0} - 0 \\&= 1.625 \times 10 ^ {0} \\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{dtSec} &= \mathrm{Lms} \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{dIsec} }{ \mathrm{Vo} } \\&= 3.839 \times 10 ^ {-5} \cdot \frac{ 1.625 \times 10 ^ {0} }{ 1.240 \times 10 ^ {1} } \\&= 5.031 \times 10 ^ {-6} \; \;\textrm{(in seconds , reminder v=Ldi/dt)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

Current in the capacitor

$i = C \dfrac{dv_{out}}{dt}$

The secondary current decreases linearly from $I_0$ to $0$ during $dt_{sec}$, where $I_0 = n \cdot I_{peak}$ is the secondary peak current:

$i(t) = I_0\left(1-\dfrac{t}{dt_{sec}}\right)$

Voltage variation on the capacitor:

$\Delta V_{out} = \dfrac{1}{C}\int_0^{dt_{sec}} i(t)\,dt$

$\Delta V_{out} = \dfrac{1}{C}\int_0^{dt_{sec}} I_0\left(1-\dfrac{t}{dt_{sec}}\right) dt$

$\Delta V_{out} = \dfrac{1}{C}\left[I_0 t - \dfrac{I_0}{2dt_{sec}}t^2\right]_0^{dt_{sec}}$

$\Delta V_{out} = \dfrac{1}{C}\left(I_0 dt_{sec} - \dfrac{I_0}{2}dt_{sec}\right)$

$\Delta V_{out} = \dfrac{I_0\,dt_{sec}}{2C}$

Using $dt_{sec} = \dfrac{L_{ms}\,\Delta I_{sec}}{V_o}$

where $V_o = V_{out} + V_d$ is the voltage across the transformer secondary (including the diode drop), and $\Delta I_{sec} = I_0$:

$\Delta V_{out} = \dfrac{I_0^2 \, L_{ms}}{2C\,V_o}$

Since $L_{ms} = \dfrac{L_m}{n^2}$ and $I_0 = n \cdot I_{peak}$:

$\Delta V_{out} = \dfrac{n^2 \, I_{peak}^2 \cdot \dfrac{L_m}{n^2}}{2C\,V_o} = \dfrac{L_m \, I_{peak}^2}{2C\,V_o}$

And

$C = \dfrac{L_m \, I_{peak}^2}{2 \, V_o \, \Delta V_{out}}$

where $V_o = V_{out} + V_d$

$ \begin{aligned} C_{out} &= \mathrm{Lm} \cdot \frac{ \left( \mathrm{Ipeak} \right) ^{ 2 } }{ 2 \cdot \mathrm{Vo} \cdot \Delta_{Vout} } \\&= 1.622 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot \frac{ \left( 2.500 \times 10 ^ {-1} \right) ^{ 2 } }{ 2 \cdot 1.240 \times 10 ^ {1} \cdot 1.200 \times 10 ^ {-1} } \\&= 3.406 \times 10 ^ {-5} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Co}_{uF} &= C_{out} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} = 0.000 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} &= 34.064 \; \;\textrm{(uF)} \end{aligned} $

For the current in the capacitor, we assume that the output current has no AC components, so all the AC ripple flows into the capacitor. Therefore, the capacitor’s peak current is equal to the peak current delivered by the secondary winding.

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Ic}_{peak} &= 1.625 \; \;\textrm{(A)} \end{aligned} $

Using the RMS definition to compute the $Is_{\text{rms}}$.

$Is_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^{T} Is(t)^2 dt} $

$Is_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^{dt_{sec}} Is_{peak}^2 \left(1 - \frac{t}{dt_{sec}}\right)^2 dt} = Is_{peak}\sqrt{\frac{dt_{sec}}{3T}}$

So in our case

$ \begin{aligned} T_{period} &= \frac{ 1 }{ \mathrm{Freq} } = \frac{ 1 }{ 1.320 \times 10 ^ {5} } &= 7.576 \times 10 ^ {-6} \; \;\textrm{(s)} \end{aligned} $
$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Is}_{rms} &= \mathrm{Is}_{peak} \cdot \sqrt { \frac{ \mathrm{dtSec} }{ 3 \cdot T_{period} } } = 1.625 \times 10 ^ {0} \cdot \sqrt { \frac{ 5.031 \times 10 ^ {-6} }{ 3 \cdot 7.576 \times 10 ^ {-6} } } &= 7.646 \times 10 ^ {-1} \; \;\textrm{(A)} \end{aligned} $

ESR estimation from $\tan(\delta)$

The dissipation factor (loss tangent) of a capacitor is defined as:

$\tan(\delta) = \dfrac{ESR}{X_C} = ESR \cdot \omega \cdot C = ESR \cdot 2\pi f \cdot C$

So:

$ESR = \dfrac{\tan(\delta)}{2\pi f \cdot C}$

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Co}_{nom_{esr}} &= 4.700 \times 10 ^ {-4} \; \;\textrm{(F, nominal capacitance)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{tol} &= 2.000 \times 10 ^ {-1} \; \;\textrm{(20\% tolerance)} \\[10pt] tan_{\delta} &= 1.200 \times 10 ^ {-1} \; \;\textrm{(dissipation factor at 120 Hz, +20°C)} \\[10pt] f_{tan} &= 120 \; \;\textrm{(Hz, frequency of tan(delta) spec)} \\[10pt] C_{min} &= \mathrm{Co}_{nom_{esr}} \cdot \left( 1 - \mathrm{tol} \right) = 4.700 \times 10 ^ {-4} \cdot \left( 1 - 2.000 \times 10 ^ {-1} \right) &= 3.760 \times 10 ^ {-4} \; \;\textrm{(F)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{ESR}_{max} &= \frac{ tan_{\delta} }{ 2 \cdot \pi \cdot f_{tan} \cdot C_{min} } = \frac{ 1.200 \times 10 ^ {-1} }{ 2 \cdot 3.142 \times 10 ^ {0} \cdot 120 \cdot 3.760 \times 10 ^ {-4} } &= 4.233 \times 10 ^ {-1} \; \;\textrm{(Ohm)} \end{aligned} $

Since we don't have ESR at heigh frequancy, we will use the simple ESR formula below:

ESR frequency scaling for electrolytics [5]: $ESR(f) = ESR(120Hz) \cdot \sqrt{\frac{120Hz}{f}}$

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{ESR}_{switching} &= \mathrm{ESR}_{max} \cdot \sqrt { \frac{ 120 }{ \mathrm{Fmin} } } = 4.233 \times 10 ^ {-1} \cdot \sqrt { \frac{ 120 }{ 1.240 \times 10 ^ {5} } } &= 1.317 \times 10 ^ {-2} \; \;\textrm{(ohm)} \end{aligned} $

The power dissipation at the capacitor:

$ \begin{aligned} P_{Co} &= \left( \mathrm{Is}_{rms} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot \mathrm{ESR}_{switching} \\&= \left( 7.646 \times 10 ^ {-1} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 1.317 \times 10 ^ {-2} \\&= 7.697 \times 10 ^ {-3} \; \;\textrm{(W)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

We don't have the thermal resistance of this capacitor, nor the maximum temperature associated with the maximum current. In the datasheet, we can see that the capacitor's Tmax is 105 °C, which corresponds to its maximum current.

Let's assume that the temperature at the maximum current is 100 °C.
So can estimate the $R_{th}$ as:

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{IrmsMax}_{datasheet} &= 1.100 \; \;\textrm{(Arms from the datasheet @ 100 kHz / +105 °C)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Tmax} &= 105 \; \;\textrm{(°C from the datasheet)} \\[10pt] T_{maxI} &= 100 \; \;\textrm{(°C, Hypothesis, max temperature at IrmsMax)} \\[10pt] P_{maxI} &= \left( \mathrm{IrmsMax}_{datasheet} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot \mathrm{ESR}_{switching} \\&= \left( 1.100 \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 0.013 \\&= 0.016 \; \;\textrm{(W)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] R_{th} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Tmax} - T_{maxI} }{ P_{maxI} } \\&= \frac{ 105 - 100 }{ 0.016 } \\&= 313.814 \; \;\textrm{(°C/W)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

For our operating point, the capacitor's T_max can be estimated using this relation.

$ \begin{aligned} T_{maxCo} &= P_{Co} \cdot R_{th} + T_{maxI} = 0.008 \cdot 313.814 + 100 &= 102.415 \; \;\textrm{(°C)} \end{aligned} $

Let's estimate the $\Delta_{Vout}$ in the worst case. We will assume that $\Delta_{Vout}$ is simply the sum of the maximum voltage caused by the capacitor and the maximum voltage caused by the ESR.

$ \begin{aligned} \Delta_{Vout_{CAPA}} &= \mathrm{Lm} \cdot \frac{ \left( \mathrm{Ipeak} \right) ^{ 2 } }{ 2 \cdot C_{min} \cdot \mathrm{Vo} } \\&= 1.622 \times 10 ^ {-3} \cdot \frac{ \left( 2.500 \times 10 ^ {-1} \right) ^{ 2 } }{ 2 \cdot 3.760 \times 10 ^ {-4} \cdot 1.240 \times 10 ^ {1} } \\&= 1.087 \times 10 ^ {-2} \; \;\textrm{(V, Pure capacitor)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \Delta_{Vout_{ESR}} &= \mathrm{Is}_{peak} \cdot \mathrm{ESR}_{switching} \\&= 1.625 \times 10 ^ {0} \cdot 1.317 \times 10 ^ {-2} \\&= 2.140 \times 10 ^ {-2} \; \;\textrm{(V, Pure resistance)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \Delta_{Vout_{WORST}} &= \Delta_{Vout_{CAPA}} + \Delta_{Vout_{ESR}} \\&= 1.087 \times 10 ^ {-2} + 2.140 \times 10 ^ {-2} \\&= 3.227 \times 10 ^ {-2} \; \;\textrm{(V)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

Above is a simplified calculation but it is a maximum of what $\Delta_{Vout}$ can be.

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{fring}_{MHz} &= \mathrm{fring} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} = 6060606.061 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} &= 6.061 \; \;\textrm{(MHz)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Llk1}_{uH} &= \mathrm{Llk1} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} = 0.000 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {6} &= 17.994 \; \;\textrm{(uH)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Prim}_{RelativeLeak} &= 100 \cdot \frac{ \mathrm{Llk1}_{uH} }{ \mathrm{Lm} } = 100 \cdot \frac{ 17.994 }{ 1650 } &= 1.091 \; \;\textrm{(\% relative leakage inductance)} \end{aligned} $

The controller operates with a nominal frequency of 132 kHz, a ±6% tolerance, and an 8 kHz jitter.
Under low‑load conditions, however, it skips cycles, making the spacing between switching periods unpredictable. To handle this, we will iterate through the data to estimate the number of periods between two successive ON instants.


Let's estimate the max-min frequencies using the tolerance and jitter:

$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Freq}_{cent_{NOM}} &= 132 \; \;\textrm{(kHz, nominal central frequency)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Freq}_{min} &= \mathrm{Freq}_{cent_{NOM}} \cdot \left( 1 - 0.06 \right) - 8 \\&= 132 \cdot \left( 1 - 0.06 \right) - 8 \\&= 116.080 \; \;\textrm{(kHz, min frequency, jitter and tolerance)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mathrm{Freq}_{max} &= \mathrm{Freq}_{cent_{NOM}} \cdot \left( 1 + 0.06 \right) + 8 \\&= 132 \cdot \left( 1 + 0.06 \right) + 8 \\&= 147.920 \; \;\textrm{(kHz, max frequency, jitter and tolerance)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} $

Let's iterate through the data to estimate the number of periods between two successive ON instants.

Regarding the max min possible frequencies, we can see that 7 periodes is the good value. so we can calculate the duty cycle now:

$ \begin{aligned} N_{periode} &= 7 \; \\[10pt] \mathrm{Freq} &= \frac{ N_{periode} }{ T \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } = \frac{ 7 }{ 53.300 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-6} } &= 131332.083 \; \;\textrm{(Hz)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{Freq}_{khz} &= \mathrm{Freq} \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} = 131332.083 \cdot 1 \times 10 ^ {-3} &= 131.332 \; \;\textrm{(kHz)} \end{aligned} $

Change the transformer airgap¶

A test using the same design at Vin = 420 V was performed, showing that the magnetizing inductance decreases from 1650 µH at 360 V to about 500 µH at 420 V. This occurs because the core becomes saturated: although the TNY‑264 limits the current, the limit depends on the current slope, and at 420 V the effective current limit exceeds 320 mA, leading to saturation.

As a solution to the core saturation, we will add an air gap to increase the current slope, reduce the voltage‑time integral, and lower the core’s peak flux density.

Vout ramp¶

We measured the Vout ramp, which is shown in the figure below:

Figure 33: Vout ramp

The ramp is composed of three segments:

  • A fast slope from 0 to 2.584 V, explained by the flyback output capacitor (470 µF).
  • A slower slope from 2.584 V to 3.878 V, caused by the flyback and buck capacitors being in parallel. Initially, the buck capacitor is charged, so the buck acts like a diode. Once Vout exceeds the capacitor’s initial voltage, the diode conducts and the two capacitors are effectively in parallel.
  • Then a second fast slope from 3.878 V, similar to the first. In this region, the buck starts converting and isolates the flyback capacitor from the buck capacitor.
